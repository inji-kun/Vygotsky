# Brainstorming

## The Rule

**No draft until the argument is explored and the human has engaged with it.**

Not "explored and approved." Explored and *engaged with*. If they say "looks good"
without engaging with the trade-offs of a framing choice, you haven't finished
brainstorming — you've just gotten a rubber stamp.

## The Process

### 1. Build Shared Understanding of the Problem Space

Don't jump to an outline. First, make sure you and the human are looking at the same
intellectual problem. Read any relevant sources. Read the diary for related concepts.

Ask the kind of questions a colleague asks when they sit down to think through a piece:
- "What's the core claim you want to make here?"
- "Who's the audience, and what do they already believe?"
- "What framing have you been leaning toward?"

**Do NOT** run through a checklist. Have a conversation. If you already know the
answer from the diary, don't re-ask — build on what's established.

### 2. Explore the Argument Space Together

Surface at least two viable framings or structural approaches. For each one:
- What it gives you — what it makes easy to argue
- What it costs you — what it forces you to defend or elide
- What it obscures — what becomes invisible under this framing

The human should be thinking about these trade-offs, not just picking from a menu.
Ask: "Which of these trade-offs matters more given your audience?" or "Where do you
think a reviewer would push back hardest?"

### 3. Present the Direction

Once you've converged, write up the direction clearly:
- **Framing**: What argument we're building and why this structure
- **Key choices**: The framing trade-offs we made and why
- **Risks**: Where the argument is weakest and how we'd know

Present it as a proposal, not a decree. The human should be able to push back on
any part of it.

### 4. Confirm Engagement, Not Just Approval

If the human rubber-stamps ("looks good", "sure", "go ahead") without engaging
with the substance:
- Pick the most consequential framing choice and make it concrete
- "Just to make sure we're aligned — we're foregrounding X over Y, which means
  a reviewer who cares about Z will find this less convincing. That feel right
  for this piece?"

This is not a quiz. It's the kind of thing you'd say to a colleague before
investing days of writing.

### 5. Record and Transition

After genuine engagement:
- Write a diary entry to `~/.vygotsky/diary/` tagged `(vygotsky-knowledge)`,
  capturing what the human demonstrated understanding of — framing choices,
  audience awareness, argument structure
- Invoke the **writing-plans** skill to translate the direction into a
  file-based plan tree

## Anti-Rationalization

If you catch yourself rationalizing why brainstorming isn't needed, STOP and read
`skills/vygotsky/reference/anti-rationalization.md` before proceeding.

Key trap for this skill: "The argument is obvious" — if it's obvious, articulating the framing takes 30 seconds. If not, you just saved days of drafting in the wrong direction.

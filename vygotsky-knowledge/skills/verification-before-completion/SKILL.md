# Verification Before Completion

## The Iron Law

**No claims without fresh verification. Evidence before assertions.**

"It should read well" is not verification. "The argument holds" without tracing it
is not verification. Summarizing a draft you haven't re-read is not verification.

## The Gate

Before claiming anything is done, walk through this:

### 1. Identify What to Verify

- Does the argument hold end-to-end? Trace the thesis through each section.
- Are sources properly integrated? Check that evidence supports claims, not just decorates them.
- Does the structure serve the reader? Walk the reader's path from opening to conclusion.
- Is the thesis actually supported? Check each major claim against its warrants.
- Are objections addressed? Identify the strongest counter-argument and check that the piece engages with it.

### 2. Do the Verification

Re-read the artifact. Not from memory. Not from your sense of what you wrote. Fresh eyes.

For each section, ask:
- What is this section's argumentative purpose?
- Does it achieve that purpose?
- Does it connect to what came before and what comes after?

### 3. Read the Output

Read the *actual* prose. Not a summary. Not your memory of writing it. The words on the page.

### 4. Confirm the Result

State what you verified and what you found:
- "The thesis is supported across all four sections — each provides independent evidence."
- "Section 3 claims X but the source only supports a weaker version of X. Flagging."
- "The transition from section 2 to 3 assumes the reader accepts Y, which hasn't been argued yet."

### 5. Then Claim

Only after steps 1-4: "This is ready."

## After Verification

If the work touched concepts the human has been building understanding of,
the verification moment is a natural theory check:
- "The piece holds together. The key thing this confirms is that the framing we
  chose in brainstorming actually carries the argument all the way through."

Don't force it. But if the verification illuminates something the human was
working to understand, say so.

## Red Flags

These mean you're about to make an unverified claim:

| Signal | Problem |
|--------|---------|
| "This should read well" | You haven't checked. |
| "The argument probably holds" | Probability is not evidence. |
| Summarizing a draft you didn't re-read | You're fabricating verification. |
| Claiming coherence from section titles alone | Titles don't argue. Read the prose. |
| "I just wrote this" | You wrote it. You haven't read it as a reader would. |

## Anti-Rationalization

If you catch yourself rationalizing why a check isn't needed, STOP and read
`skills/vygotsky/reference/anti-rationalization.md` before proceeding.

Key trap for this skill: "I just wrote it, I know what it says" — you know what you intended. You don't know what you produced until you read it.
